    AGILE METHODOLOGY

    The Agile Web design process gives a framework to deliver exceptional websites on budget and on time and ensure optimal usage of resources. This framework is important in a digital world where web design is important to achieve a successful digital marketing strategy. [1]

    ENHANCING USER EXPERIENCE

    In the past, websites were seen as digital fancy business cards. Today, however, they are immersive digital experiences that create wealth - in some cases, being the only revenue generation source (e.g., e-commerce).

    When designing websites, if the user were to only have 15 minutes to view your content, two-thirds of them would visit an aesthetically pleasing website instead.
    Users are more likely to leave your website if it's designed poorly, with just under half of users in a survey saying that design is the most crucial part in determining a brand's trustworthiness.

    Agile web design process is an evolution of a mix of waterfall and traditional design approaches and considers the extremely competitive and dynamic online environment.
    This section will dive deep into the web design process, underscoring key departures from other process types but also showing the benefits of using it. [1]

    THE AGILE METHODOLOGY DEFINED


    The agile methodology is a SCRUM-based incremental, iterative way to software development or web design that prioritizes continuous delivery over linear, step-based milestones, and rigid objectives. In this approach, the designers work in short sprints (for one to two weeks) to quickly test and prototype new features at every step of the project.


    The agile web design process directs the designers to release their designs and get real-time feedback from the users and stakeholders early, this provides a chance to make changes quickly. The result of an agile web design development methodology is an agile project that includes detailed feedback whilst sticking to short timelines. [1]

    PRINCIPLES OF THE AGILE WEB DESIGN PROCESS

    During the process, priorities and options must be weighted, with each choice impeding or speeding up the workflow.
    Though it's not always clear what approach is the best, the agilemethod offers four core principles that determine what to prioritize during the process. [1]

    • Interactions and individuals over tools and processes
    • Working software over comprehensive documentation
    • Customer collaboration over contract negotiation
    • Responding to change over following a plan

    AGILE vs. TRADITONAL vs. WATERFALL WEB DESIGN PROCESSES


    Three options are on the table in any web design scenario: traditional, waterfall or agile web design. Though traditional web development is archaic, some companies still choose to use it, often when website design changes are internal. Agile and waterfall fall under modern web design, both with their unique nuances. In the picture you can see the differences between the processes. [1]


    AGILE vs. TRADITONAL AND WATERFALL

    Traditional website design is often an internally-driven process. A company hires a web design agency. However, they call all the shots regarding functionality, testing, design, execution, etc. This type of design is outdated since it doesn't consider the customer at all. The main difference between agile and traditional is that agile is user-centred whilst traditional is company-centred.

    The waterfall method for designing websites is a future-driven process that tackles one step at a time, hence the waterfall or cascading motif. Though this approach is goal-driven, it falls in its lack of flexibility. The main risks are that any issues early on in the process hold up the rest of the steps, leading to project delays. The main difference between agile and waterfall is that agile is iterative and flexible, whilst a waterfall is rigid and linear. [1]

    THE AGILE WEB DESIGN PROCESS





    The agile web development process has multiple steps that usually co-occur. The process relies on teamwork, and early team meetings are crucial for the project's success. Instead of following a long design process, agile involves sprints -- each with a timeline and unique goal.

    The agile approach usually includes these steps: [1]

    1. Goals
      The first step is to set clear goals. What is the team trying to achieve with their website? What needs to be included on the site? Who is the targeted audience? Answering these questions helps to create clear goals for the team.
    2. Ideation
      The second step is to come up with ideas for meeting the goals. The assembled development and design team brainstorms with other stakeholders to explore innovative and new ideas for the project.
    3. Plan
      The third step is to plan how to make the first and second steps reality. It involves creating a plan for the project covering user wireframes, flows, and prototypes to help the team visualize the website. It is also essential to keep the customers in mind but also, if possible, involve during this step to ensure that the final product meets their needs.
    4. Sprint
      A sprint means having a short, focused period (usually 2-4 weeks) where the team works on a specific goal. This goal could be anything from adding new features or designing the home page. During the sprints, staying focused is crucial.
    5. Iterate
      After each sprint, the team takes a step back and subjects the outcome of the sprint to user-centric analysis, feedback, and testing. Data collected from the functionality and feedback metrics is placed back into the process whether this informs the team whether to continue or tweak the design more. During this step, the focus is to not get stuck and keep the progression going.
    6. Production
      Once all the goals for the project have been met, the design is usually production-ready and can move on further to the development phase. This might seem like the end of the process. However, the concept of agile assumes that this step can go backwards to any previous steps, depending on any new information arising.

    BENEFITS OF USING AGILE WEB DESIGN





    There are many benefits to using this process. Some of these benefits include: [1]

    1. Transparency
      The main benefits of agile is that it is a transparent process. Every single one of the team members knows what needs to be done and when, helping each other and keeping everyone on track.
    2. Faster Launch
      Since agile involves sprints, the project tends to move along faster since the sprints have a specific timeline and goals.
    3. Improved Quality and Customer Satisfaction
      Agile involves getting feedback from the users early on product helps with improving the final products quality, leading to customer satisfaction rising.
    4. Focus on Core Project Deliverables
      An advantage of agile web design is that it assists the team to focus on the most crucial aspects of the project, allowing for better use of results and resources in a higher-quality product.
    5. Reduced Risk
      Agile inherently reduces the risk since changes can be made quickly based on the feedback from the user, this helps avoid costly mistakes and keep the project on track.
    6. Cost Savings
      Agile web design can also lead to cost savings due to an adoptable and flexible process structure. The team can make changes easily and quickly without a high cost.
    7. Easy Adaptation to Change
      Agile is all about flexibility, it's easy to adapt to new requirements, circumstances, or data. This is especially beneficial when run as a data-driven process.
    8. Better Communication
      Better communication is a hallmark of the agile web design process. It encourages open communication in the team, between the customer, and stakeholders throughout the process. This helps avoid misunderstandings in communication and keep everyone on the same page.

    AGILE WEB DESIGN LIMITATIONS



    Despite the benefits, there is also some limitations to using an agile web design process: [1]

    1. Requires Agile Expertise
      Agile process requires a team led by a certified SCRUM master that embraces and understands the agile methodology. Christening and recruiting a team an agile team can do more harm than good since the process will be marred with delays, misunderstandings, and general confusion.
    2. Not Always Suitable for Large Projects
      Agile web design can be challenging to scale up in larger projects since the process relies heavily on the user's feedback. In large projects with many stakeholders, it can be challenging to keep soliciting input from many different people.
    3. It can lead to Scope Creep
      One danger with agile is that sometimes it can lead to scope creep (the projects time increasing over time), especially when a team continues to make changes based on the feedback from the users without considering the overall impact on the project. A never-ending backlog and scope creep are the leading reasons why projects take longer time than expected and go over budget.
