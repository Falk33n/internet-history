    HISTORY OF THE INTERNET

    The internet is a big part of our daily lives now that it seems like it's been here forever. The history of the internet began in the 1950s. Which later on evolved into what we know it as today. In the beginning, it was only available for scientists and scholars, but today the internet is open for everyone to enjoy. This is the history of the internet. [1]

    Ordering an uber, ordering food, or sharing your pictures - we all do it. Our work halts if we lose our internet connection. We are so used to the internet that we can't imagine life before the internet. But how was it created, who created it, and why? Let us tell you the story of the internet, from the early days to what we know about the internet as today.

    The invention of the computer network ARPANET, email, DNS, IRC, the launch of AOL, the first ISPs, the invention of Wi-Fi, the internet becoming available to the public. All of these brought us to where we are today. Continue reading to learn about these innovations. [1]

    WHAT IS THE INTERNET?

    The internet is a network of devices connected globally that we use to share information. It is composed of smaller networks such as businesses, governments, educational institutions and individuals. These networks connect through infrastructure, satellites and wireless connections. But when was the first network created? [1]

    1950s-1980s


    In the 1950s, US defense, research, and educational institutions created the first computer network for sharing information. At that time, computers could only be connected if they were in the same location. And the ability to share data was limited. However, the creation of ARPANET changed that. In the 1980s, scientists created the start of what later on would become the internet we know as of today. [1]

    ARPANET — THE BEGINNING OF THE INTERNET (1969)

    In 1969, in the middle of the Cold War, the Pentagon's Advanced Research Project Agency (ARPA) started a project called ARPANET whose goal was to create a network for computers to communicate with each other over long distances. ARPANET created a new networking system, called packet switching. This system made it possible for computers to send messages - private data packages - around the network.

    ARPANET connects computers across universities. Throughout the 70s, ARPANET included more and more universities, research centers and government institutions. In the 80s, ARPANET had completed its goal and was phased out. The remaining networks are the precursors of the internet today. [1]

    THE FIRST EMAIL (1971)

    The first message that ARPANET sent from one computer to another in 1969 was a far cry from an email as we know it. The idea of sending an instant message from one device to another became so appealing that specialists developed more complex protocols for sending them. However, one thing remains - how do you indicate where the message is supposed to go to? Ray Tomlinson, an American programmer working at ARPANET, came up with the answer - the “@” symbol. In 1971, Tomlinson proposed to his co-workers to seperate the user's name from the receiving computer with an “@”, addressing the messages' “username@name of the computer. This is how we've addressed emails ever since.

    In the beginning, email was used to communicate within internal networks of universities. But the creation of it was so useful that programmers started developing a way to send emails to computers outside of the internal network. As the internet grew, it became an integral part of professional communication.

    Email began spreading in the 1990s with the rise of internet service providers (ISPs) offering free email services to people. Web-based email services such as Hotmail and Yahoo Mail contributed to email's popularity. At the beginning of the 2000, email had already become a daily mode of communication. [1]

    ARPANET SWITCHES TO TCP/IP (1983-1985)

    Until 1983, ARPANET computers used NCP (Network Control Protocol) to communicate with one another. Though NCP had limits and was not suitable for connecting different types of computer networks. To solve this, developers created the TCP/IP (Transmission Control Protocol/Internet Protocol). TCP/IP was more flexible and could connect to different types of computer networks, which in return enabled better communication.

    The transition from NCP to TCP/IP was gradual since the network infrastructure was in need of an update. Special translators, known as protocol gateways, were created in aid of computers that still used NCP, to communicate with those who were using TCP/IP. [1]

    DNS COMES INTO PLAY (1985)

    To understand the importance of DNS, let's look at what an IP address is. An IP address, or an internet protocol address, is a unique string of characters that identifies each separate computer communicating over the internet.

    In the early days, network hosts (computers that were communicating with one another on the network) were identified by using numerical IP addresses, for example, 192.0.2.1. With time, the internet grew and it became impractical to remember all IP addresses. This is where DNS (Domain Name System) comes to play.

    DNS was created back in 1984 to translate human-readable domain names into IP addresses. It acts like an address book. It's easier to remember domain names rather than complicated strings of numbers. [1]

    THE LAUNCH OF AOL (1985)

    AOL (formerly known as America Online) started back in 1985 and rebranded to AOL in 1991. It was an online service provider that was the start of the consumer internet. AOL provided easy-to-access online platforms which introduced the internet to millions of people.

    Back in time, mostly businesses, educational and government institutions used the internet. In the 90s, AOL made the internet available by providing users with dial-up internet access, which allowed people at home to connect using their phone lines. Online chat rooms and communities are flooded with users, creating social interactions online. The internet started to become what it is today. [1]

    THE IRC FOR DIRECT MESSAGING (1988)

    With email on the rise, the demand for live messaging came, and IRC (Internet Relay Chat) fulfilled that. IRC is a real-time communication protocol and text-based chat system that allows for direct messaging between people.

    In 1988, Jarko Oikarinen, a Finnish computer scientist, created the IRC and, by that, enabled group communication in discussion forums, private messaging, data transfer, and file sharing among users in different places. IRC presented the concept of channels where users could join and participate in discussions. [1]

    THE BEGINNING OF THE WORLD WIDE WEB (1989)

    The difference between the web (or the World Wide Web, WWW) and the internet is that the web is a system of interconnected hypertext documents, media, and resources, whilst the internet is a global network of interconnected computers that are used to access that system, utilizing technologies like HTTP and HTML. But who had the brain to create the technologies that were the start of the web as today?

    In 1989, Tim Berners-Lee, working at CERN (the European Organization for Nuclear Research), proposed to his co-workers to create a networked hypertext system to navigate through linked documents stored on CERN's computers. His proposal outlined the key parts of the World Wide Web: [1]

    • The Uniform Resource Identifier (URI) provides a standardized way to identify resources on the web
    • The Hypertext Transfer Protocol (HTTP) for transferring data over the internet.
    • The Hypertext Markup Language (HTML) provides structure and formatting of documents, which enables the displaying of text, images, and links.
    • The distribution of a system for decentralized information to be accessed from any computer that is connected


    Berner-Lee's approach removed the need for centralized servers and allowed the growth of the web. His proposals laid the grounds for the interconnected and accessible web we know today. [1]

    THE FIRST COMMERCIAL DIAL-UP ISP (1989)

    There is a debate about what commercial internet service provider (ISP) was the first one, but one called "The World" was among the earliest ones which started serving customers back in 1989. Established by Joel Furr, it started by providing direct connection to the internet in the San Francisco Bay area. Until then, the internet was mainly used by military, research, and educational institutions and businesses. The world gives users internet access to home.

    The World offered their services for a fee that provided user-friendly tools and interfaces for navigating WWW, including one of the first web browsers, The Web Explorer. The World contributed greatly world the growth of the internet by offering services like email, newsgroups, online chats, and file sharing for a larger audience. [1]

    1990s-2009


    In the 1990s-2019 we saw a rapid expansion of the internet, mainly because of multiple ISPs offering their services not only to the scientific and academic community, but also to businesses and home users. This is what made the internet so widespread that it is today. These decades saw a lot of firsts: the first photo, the first web page, the first webcam, the first social media platform, the invention of Wi-Fi and Google. [1]

    THE FIRST SEARCH ENGINE (1990)

    In 1990, Alan Emtage, from Monreal, Canada, created the first search engine, Archie. Back then, the internet mainly consisted of FTP sites where users could download files. Archie created an index of files on FTP (File Transfer Protocol) sites. Archie would scan FTP servers and create databases of file names that were searchable. The users would search for a specific file by their names.

    Archie could, however, not search for content within web pages and would index only specific file format. Archie laid the grounds for more advanced search engines of the 90s such as Gopher, Veronika, and Jughead, that began to index content on the web. These all led to landmark search engines, such as Web Crawler (1994), Lycos (1994), Alta Vista ( 1995), and eventually Google (1999). [1]

    THE FIRST WEBPAGE (1991)

    In 1991, Tim Berners-Lee, the creator of the World Wide Web, created yet another piece of technology - the first web page. The web page had a simple design and consisted of only plain text. The web page's content was an overview of the WWW project, an explanation of hypertext, and instructions on how to create web pages by clicking on text and images. The address (or URL) on the first web page was http://info.cern.ch/hypertext/WWW/TheProject.html, and you can still access it today! [1]

    THE FIRST WEBCAM (1991)

    In 1991, a team consisting of researchers led by Quentin Stafford-Fraser and Paul Jardetzsky set up the first webcam in the laboratory of the University of Cambridge. The camera was called the "coffee pot cam" by the creators since the purpose of the camera was to provide a live video feed of the coffee pot in the coffee room so that the researchers could see if the pot was full or not. The webcam allowed the users to check the status of the coffee pot by viewing images uploaded to a web page at regular intervals. The live feed was a black and white image with a resolution of 128x128 pixels.

    With the invention of this webcam, the coffee pot became a global attraction. The researchers eventually retired it in 2001 and auctioned the webcam off on eBay. [1]

    FIRST PHOTO UPLOADED ON THE INTERNET (1992)

    No one can say for sure which photo was truly the first on the internet - it all depends on the context we apply to the photos. However, in 1992, Tim Berners-Lee shared a photo on the internet which was for many years considered to be the first one. The photo was of four women, the members of the parody musical group called Les Horribles Cernettes, or the Horrible CERN Girls. The photo was in the basic GIF (Graphics Interchange Format) format, which was commonly used for images at that time. Even if this picture were not to be the first ever, it still played a big role in a new era of sharing visual content online. [1]

    GOVERNMENT PARTICIPATION (1993)

    In the early 1990s, the internet was becoming more and more popular and started to affect society. This led to the governments joining in. In 1993, US president Bill Clinton introduced the National Information Infrastructure (NII), an initiative to develop policies and necessary infrastructure to support the growth of the internet, ensure universal access, and address issues like privacy, and security. This initiative laid the grounds for the beginning of increased governmental participation regarding the internet.

    International organizations also joined in. In 1992, the United Nations organized the World Summit on the Information Society (WSIS) that brought together governments, international organizations, and members of society to shape policies for the information age. That same year, an international nonprofit organization, the Internet Society, was founded. Regional and international organizations, including the European Union, the Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development, and the International Telecommunication Union, also began discussing policies regarding the internet.

    The early 1990s was a period when governments recognized the internet's implications beyond technical infrastructure and started to work on more policies. Frameworks were established for governance, regulation, and international cooperation to ensure internet security. [1]

    FIRST SECURE ECOMMERCE TRANSACTION (1994)

    The first secure online transaction was made back in 1994, beginning a new eurozone in the history of e-commerce. A man named Phil Brandenberger from Philadelphia used the Netscape Navigator web browser to buy a CD from the NetMarket website, which is an online retailer founded by Daniel Kohn. The CD was Sting's album "Ten Summoner's Tales" and costed $12.48 plus $4.95 in shipping.

    This transaction was made using a security protocol called the Secure Sockets Layer (SSL), developed by the Netscape Communications Corporation. The SSL encrypted credit card information and ensured a secure transfer of data between the seller and buyer. It provided a level of security and trust needed for online transactions.

    There had been other online transactions before this. In 1992, the Internet Shopping Network (INS) platform started selling products ranging from flowers to books. However, the Sting CD represents an important milestone in a secure way of online shopping. [1]

    THE FIRST SOCIAL MEDIA PLATFORM (1996)

    Six Degrees is considered the first social media platform. Created in 1996 by Andrew Weinreich, this social networking site offered features like customizable profiles, friends lists, and messaging. It lets you browse other user's profiles, and join forums and groups.

    Though Six Degrees had a small user base - only around 1 million users - compared to today's social networks, like Facebook (launched in 2004). Personal information on your profile was limited, there was no real-time chat or video call functions, and you could not upload videos or photos. Still, Six Degrees laid the grounds for social media platforms as we know of today. [1]

    THE INVENTION OF Wi-Fi (1997)

    Wireless fidelity (or simply Wi-Fi) is a technology that allows devices to connect to the internet wirelessly over a local area network (LAN). In 1997, a team of researchers at the NRC Corporation, led by Vic Hayes, created the first wireless local area network (WLAN) standard - the IEEE 802.11. This standard defined the protocols for wireless communication, and a framework for the creation of Wi-Fi technology. Our Wi-Fi technology today is based on the IEEE 802.11 standard.

    There were numerous versions of IEEE 802.11, each better than the previous one. The Wi-Fi Alliance, a non-profit organization established in 1999, has made Wi-Fi available to the public by certifying devices that adhere to the IEEE 802.11 standards and making sure of interoperability between different manufacturers' products. To make the technical name IEEE 802.11 sound better to consumers, marketing consultants for the Wi-Fi Alliance created the term "Wi-Fi". The term is based on the term "Hi-FI" (used for high-quality audio equipment) and wireless. [1]

    HERE COMES GOOGLE (1998)

    In 1998, Larry Page and Sergey Brin, students at Stanford University, created the Google search engine. As students, they worked together on a research project aimed at analyzing and organizing the large amounts of information on the web. Their breakthrough was Page's development of a concept called PageRank - an algorithm that ranks web pages according to their importance. This approach made it possible for them to identify more relevant search results.

    In 1997, Page and Brin registered the domain name google.com, referring to the word "googol", which means the number 1 followed by 100 zeros Google was a metaphor to show the massive amount of information available on the web. One year later, they created a private company named Google, in Menlo Park, California.

    Google expanded more and launched several innovative products, such as Google AdWords (now Google Ads), Google Images, Google Maps, Gmail, Google Docs, Google Drive, and many more. Today, Google is the most popular and used search engine globally. [1]

    2000-2009


    The 2000s was an era of internet hype; firstly, social networks, secondly, user-driven content creation. The internet became popular not just with society but also among investors, who inflated the dot-com bubble until it eventually burst in the early 2000s. MySpace, Wikipedia, and Digg gained momentum. Expressing your opinions online really has become great. The first smartphones brought the WWW into our pockets and the developer's focus shifted to the mobile web.[1]

    THE BURST OF THE DOT-COM BUBBLE (2000)

    The dot-com bubble was a stock market bubble which burst in the early 2000s, impacting internet and technology-related stocks of the so-called dot-com companies, primarily based in the United States.

    In the late 1990s, seeing the potential of the internet and an eagerness to capitalize on it, venture capitalists and private investors poured money into the hyped-up industry, and stock prices of dot-com companies skyrocketed. Most of these companies would not generate a sustainable revenue. This led to the stock market decline

    Over-evaluation was one of the top reasons why the dot-com bubble burst. Most of the companies in the dot-com bubble were valued too high based on their future expectations rather than present-day reality. Investors started to sell off their stocks, funding eventually dried up and that led to job losses and an economic downturn. Eventually, internet companies recovered and ensured more sustainable growth. [1]

    THE LAUNCH OF WIKIPEDIA (2001)

    Wikipedia is one of the most used online encyclopedias. It was created back in 2001 by Jimmy Wales and Larry Sanger, authors of an earlier web-based encyclopedia called Nupedia which hosted expert-written articles. Nupedia saw slow progress and this inspired the authors to develop a way to create content faster. So they came up with the concept of a collaborative, community-driven website.

    Wikipedia allows the users to edit, add and modify the content using a simple text-coding system. As long as you have internet access, you can contribute by writing or editing articles. The editors are encouraged to quote only reliable sources and have a neutral point of view.

    Soon after the launch, many individuals volunteered in the project, this turned Wikipedia into a valuable resource for research. Today, Wikipedia contains millions of articles, covering a wide range of topics. Wikipedia is also available in over 300 languages. [1]

    MySPACE EXPLODES (2003)

    MySpace is one of the biggest social networking platforms, launched back in 2003 by Tom Anderson and Chris DeWolfe. Users of MySpace can create personalized profiles, connect with family and friends, and share photos or music.

    A MySpace feature at that time was that users could customize their profiles through HTML and CSS coding. The users could create unique online identities. This also meant that profile pages were less streamlined than others, at that time, at that time.

    MySpace also plays a role in the music industry by providing the platform needed for independent and upcoming artists to share their music. Over 10,000 songs were uploaded daily in its peak years.

    MySpace's growth peaked back in 2006, surpassing even Google as the most-visited site in the US. In 2008, Facebook overtook MySpace's lead, leaving MySpace struggling to maintain its user base. In internet history, MySpace is important for making the concept of connecting over the internet with one another popular. Today, MySpace is still used by several million people. [1]

    DIGG TAKES THE LEAD (2004)

    Digg was a social news website that allowed the users to share newsworthy content. It was created back in 2004 by its founders Kevon Rose, Owen Byrne, Ron Gorodetzky and Jay Adelson. Digg offered a novel type of content for content discovery by enabling users to submit links to blog posts, images, videos, and news articles. The site was based on how the community voted on the content, making the most popular news rise to the top of the wep page. This model of community-curated content was called "crowdsurfing".

    In 2008 Digg became the most visited website on the internet. The voting system that Digg invented was new at the time. This empowered the users to have a direct impact on the content. It was also a door for smaller publishers to get their news out. Digg unfortunately dug its own grave in 2010 by redesigning the website by adding an algorithmic content ranking system, which led to user backlash. Most users moved on to other websites, such as Reddit. [1]

    iPHONES AND THE MOBILE WEB (2007)

    In 2007, the iPhone launched. This changed the landscape of the mobile web. The iPhone offered a user-friendly multi-touch interface which immediately popularized touchscreen smartphones, encouraging competitors to develop similar devices. Android came up with their version of it just a year later.

    The iPhone also provided users with a powerful web browser, Safari, that allowed the users to browse the internet on their phones, changing the limited experience to a desktop-like one. This has led to web developers focusing on responsive designs, ensuring that users can access websites on their phones with different screen sizes. Over time, HTML, CSS3 and JavaScript enabled the creation of a more interactive mobile web experience. For the users, this meant a much easier and enjoyable way to access the internet.

    Apple also created an app called the App Store, which allowed third-party app developers to distribute applications for the iPhone, paving for the new era of mobile software development. Basically, Apple introduced a market for developers to present their apps. [1]

    THE INTRODUCTION OF BITCOIN (2009)

    Back in 2008, the first cryptocurrency, Bitcoin, was invented by a programmer or group of programmers under the pseudonym Satoshi Nakamoto, who published a whitepaper outlining Bitcoin's concept and framework. It wasn't until 2009 became functional, with the mining of the first block, known as the "Genesis block".

    Bitcoin is a cryptocurrency operating outside the control of authorities like governments. This digital asset was one step ahead of its predecessors since it introduced a decentralized ledger called blockchain, recording all transactions made with Bitcoin and keeping a record of history and ownership, which eliminated double-spending issues. The mechanism used for validating network participants, ensured a limited supply of 21 million coins, and pseudonymized all transactions, preventing fraudulent transactions.

    Its success transformed the whole financial landscape and inspired other creators to develop alternative cryptocurrencies. Bitcoin laid the grounds for a broader adoption of blockchain technologies in other industries. [1]

    ICANN POLICY CHANGES (2009)

    The Internet Corporation for Assigned Names and Numbers (ICANN) is a non-profit organization that helps keep the internet secure, stable and unified globally by coordinating the Internet's domain name system (DNS), IP address allocation, and protocol parameter assignment. ICANN is a partnership consisting of people all around the world, formed back in 1988 and operates under the principles of multi-stakeholder governance.

    In 2009, ICANN changed several policies that significantly impacted domain name registration but also the general management of the internet:

    • The new generic top-level domain (gTLD) program that increased the stability of domain name options, which allowed for customized domain extensions.
    • The Fast-track Process for Internationalized Domain Names (IDNs) introduced domain names that included non-Latin characters, which allowed for greater linguistic inclusivity on the internet.
    • A revised version of the Registrar Accreditation Agreement (RAA) enhanced consumer security, protection, accountability, and transparency within the domain name registration system.
    • Improved WHOIS (a publicly accessible database containing information about domain name registrants) accuracy requirements fought off spam, intellectual property infringement, and domain name abuse.


    These changes made the domain name registration process more available, contributing to linguistic inclusions and diversity on the internet, but also, most importantly, protecting the interests of internet users. [1]

    2010-2019


    Social media platforms increased in popularity and expanded their user base, smart technologies evolved, and e-commerce partly replaced normal shopping. This era saw the rise of influencers, streaming services, mobile games, viral trends, and the creation of AI technologies, setting the foundation for the future. [1]

    SOCIAL MEDIA

    Social media platforms expanded popular and expanded their features [1] :

    • Facebook dominated the landscape of social media and went on to acquire both Instagram and WhatsApp.
    • Twitter, a microblogging platform, had millions of users. They engaged them with news and discussion in its 280-character "tweets".
    • Instagram went on to become the leading video and photo sharing platform, intended for celebrities, influencers and individuals.
    • Snapchat introduced the world to disappearing photos and video messages and stories. These were later adopted by many other social media platforms.
    • Pintarest has become the leading platform for visual discovery, a place where you can share images related to the user's interests.
    • LinkedIn became the most popular social networking platform for professionals, headhunters and job seekers.

    SMART TECHNOLOGIES

    The inventions of smartphones contributed to the expansion of the mobile internet. App stores like Google Play and App Store grew exponentially. Apps can offer everything from communication to social networking and entertainment. Some mobile games also went viral, such as Candy Crush Saga, Pokémon Go and Angry Birds. [1]

    E-COMMERCE AND ONLINE ENTERTAINMENT

    E-commerce also grew during this era, platforms such as eBay, Amazon, and Alibaba increased its usage, while subscription-based streaming services like Hulu, Amazon Prime Video, Disney+ and Netflix disrupted the traditional TV industry.

    YouTube led the race among other platforms for user-generated content. It introduced the internet to live streams and a Partner Program that lets content creators monetize their videos and gain revenue through viewer memberships, ads, merchandise, and sponsored content. Being an influencer or youtuber with an online presence on different social networks became a career. Influencer marketing has become a prominent way for businesses to reach the targeted audience. [1]

    THE INTERNET DURING THE PANDEMIC AND NOW


    Before Covid-19 we relied on the internet every day, but after Covid-19 began shutting down the world, the usage of the internet grew even greater. Covid-19 hit at the end of 2019 and led to lockdowns around the world throughout 2020 and 2021. This resulted in continuous remote work, social distancing, and our social lives moving to the web. [1]

    REMOTE WORK AND LEARNING

    Throughout the pandemic, people learned, worked, communicated, and entertained themselves from home, using online tools. Work moved to platforms like Microsoft Teams, Zoom, and Google Meet. Educational institutions shifted to e-learning platforms like Microsoft Teams or Google Classroom for education. Virtual webinars and classrooms became the new norm. [1]

    ONLINE ENTERTAINMENT

    Entertainment also got focused on being more online. Streaming services experienced an increase in users, and online gaming thrived. Suffering from social isolation, people started to come to social media platforms more and more to stay up to date with family and friends. [1]

    CYBERSECURITY

    This large dependence on the internet increased the cybercrime rates all over the internet. This highlighted the importance of cybersecurity. Technologies like antimalware software and antivirus, prevention systems and intrusion detection, multi-factor authentication, and VPNs have become a necessity for users of the internet. [1]

    WHAT THE FUTURE MAY HOLD

    Since internet speed has increased with the wide adoption of 5G, global connectivity is bound to grow. There are predictions that the Internet of Things will grow as well, connecting even more devices and enabling smart homes, vehicles, cities, and industries. The increase in connectivity and usage of smart devices is bound to create better security and privacy solutions.

    People's work and entertainment processes are also expected to change with technologies such as augmented reality and virtual reality transforming the way we consume content, work, and interact on the web. And with AI on the rise, we'll see what future the internet brings us. [1]
